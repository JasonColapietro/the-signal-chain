# Appendix B - A Visual Timeline of Amps and Effects

Lay the whole story end to end and a shape appears. A single horseshoe magnet wrapped in wire becomes, within a human lifetime, a fingernail of silicon running mathematics fast enough to fool a working professional's ears. The arc from the **Rickenbacker Frying Pan** to the **Neural DSP Quad Cortex** is not a straight climb toward "better" -- it is a series of accidents, frustrations, and stubborn personalities, each one solving the previous generation's problem and quietly creating the next. A timeline flattens that drama into dates, but read the dates with the stories already in your ears and you can hear the century move: the cardboard rasp of an overdriven valve in 1951, the synthetic snarl of germanium in 1962, the glassy chime of cathode-biased EL84s, the digital convolution of a real cabinet's impulse response captured in a quiet room.

A word on how to read what follows. Each entry gives a year, an em dash, the milestone, an em dash, and a one-line note on why it matters. Dates for mass-produced gear are knowable and I have kept them honest; where a year is genuinely soft -- a slow rollout, a disputed "first," a model that trickled out before its official announcement -- I have marked it `c.` (circa) or flagged the dispute in the note. Treat the round numbers in the decade headings as conveniences, not gospel. The electric guitar was built by tinkerers, and tinkerers rarely keep good records.

## The 1930s

The instrument has to exist before its amplifier can matter. The decade's achievement is simply proving that a magnetic pickup -- a coil of wire sensing a vibrating steel string and turning it into a tiny alternating voltage -- could be loud, useful, and musical.

- **1931** -- *Rickenbacker "Frying Pan" (A-22/A-25)* -- the first commercially produced electric guitar; its horseshoe-magnet pickup proves the magnetic-coil principle that every entry below depends on.
- **1932** -- *Ro-Pat-In / Electro String amplifier* -- among the earliest purpose-built guitar amps, a small field-coil speaker box made to partner the Frying Pan rather than a radio repurposed by accident.
- **1936** -- *Gibson ES-150* -- the first widely successful "Spanish" electric archtop; its bar pickup, later nicknamed the "Charlie Christian" pickup, gave the new music of amplified single-note jazz its founding voice.
- **1939** -- *Gibson EH-185 amplifier* -- a comparatively powerful pre-war combo whose clean headroom set the template Leo Fender would study and improve.

## The 1940s

War swallowed the middle of the decade; vacuum tubes went to radar and radios, not guitar amps. What survives is the groundwork -- and, at the very end, a small Southern California shop that would change the trade.

- **1946** -- *Fender Electric Instrument Company founded* -- Leo Fender turns from radio repair to building amplifiers, treating the amp as a serious instrument in its own right.
- **c. 1948** -- *Fender "Woodie" / early Princeton and Deluxe* -- small tweed-era combos in wooden cabinets establish Fender's house sound: bright, immediate, eager to break up.

## The 1950s

This is the decade tone is born twice -- first as glorious clean power, then as the happy accident of distortion. The defining circuit is the cathode-biased, class-AB push-pull power section feeding a guitar speaker hard enough that the tubes themselves begin to clip.

- **1951** -- *Fender "wide-panel" tweed Bassman (5B6)* -- built for the new Precision Bass, but guitarists discover that a cranked Bassman has a singing, harmonically rich overdrive nobody designed on purpose.
- **1954** -- *Fender Stratocaster* (and the **5C8/5D8 tweed era continues**) -- the guitar arrives that will define the platform; meanwhile the tweed lineup matures toward its sweetest voicing.
- **1955** -- *Tweed Deluxe (5E3)* -- a low-wattage, two-knob combo whose touch-sensitive breakup becomes the benchmark for "amp-only" overdrive for the next seventy years.
- **1958** -- *Fender 5F6-A tweed Bassman* -- four 10-inch speakers, two 5881/6L6 output tubes, a long-tailed-pair phase inverter; the single most copied amplifier circuit in history.
- **1959** -- *Fender "high-power" tweed Twin* -- roughly 80 watts of clean before grit; the loud, glassy headroom that would anchor country, surf, and clean funk.

## The 1960s

Everything happens at once. America cleans up and gets louder; Britain learns from a borrowed Bassman and reinvents it twice; and a tiny stomp-box industry is born when engineers learn to put distortion, modulation, and echo in a box on the floor. The harmonic logic underneath the era is the **overtone series** -- when a string vibrates, it sounds not only its fundamental but a stack of quieter integer multiples (octave, fifth, octave, major third...). Overdriving an amp or a fuzz multiplies and reshuffles those overtones, which is exactly why distortion makes a power chord sound "thick" rather than merely loud.

- **1960** -- *Fender "brownface" / dawn of blackface design* -- fixed-bias, cleaner, more headroom; the move away from tweed grit toward the scooped, sparkling clean that defines "Fender clean."
- **1962** -- *Maestro FZ-1 Fuzz-Tone* -- the first mass-marketed fuzz pedal; its splatty, transistor-driven distortion sounds like a torn speaker because, in spirit, that is what it imitates.
- **1962** -- *Vox AC30 "Top Boost"* -- the Top Boost circuit (an extra ECC83 gain/tone stage) gives the cathode-biased, EL84-powered AC30 its chiming high end and vocal midrange; the sound of the British Invasion.
- **1965** -- *Marshall JTM45* -- Jim Marshall and Ken Bran build, essentially, a 5F6-A Bassman with British parts; KT66 power tubes and a different transformer push it toward a darker, more aggressive roar.
- **1965** -- *Sola Sound Tone Bender MkI* -- a British answer to the FZ-1; thicker and more sustaining, the bridge between the Maestro and the Fuzz Face.
- **1966** -- *Arbiter Fuzz Face* -- two germanium transistors in a round enclosure; touch-responsive, cleaning up with the guitar's volume knob, it becomes Hendrix's signature gain stage.
- **1966** -- *Marshall 1959 "Plexi" Super Lead (100W) and 1987 (50W)* -- EL34 power tubes and no negative-feedback tinkering yield the canonical British stack crunch.
- **1967** -- *Vox / Warwick Electronics Clyde McCoy wah* -- the first wah pedal, a swept band-pass filter on a treadle; a year later the line becomes the **Vox/Thomas Organ Cry Baby**.
- **1968** -- *Uni-Vibe (Shin-ei / Univox)* -- four staggered phase stages meant to fake a rotating Leslie speaker; it misses that target and lands on its own seasick, watery shimmer instead.
- **1969** -- *Electro-Harmonix Big Muff Pi* -- four cascaded transistor clipping stages with a scooped midrange; endless violin-like sustain and a wall of fuzz, soon a Gilmour staple.

## The 1970s

The amplifiers grow up and grow angry. Players who had been overdriving the *power* section now want gain earlier in the chain, at lower volume; the answer is cascaded *preamp* gain, and it remakes rock. Meanwhile the pedalboard fills with the modulation and overdrive shapes still in print today. Much of the decade's lead playing lives in the **minor pentatonic** and **blues scale** -- the five-note minor pattern (root, b3, 4, 5, b7) with a chromatic b5 "blue note" slipped between the 4 and 5 -- because distortion rewards the strong, vocal intervals of those scales and punishes harmonic clutter.

- **1971** -- *Mesa/Boogie Mark I* -- Randall Smith's hot-rodded, cascaded-gain Princeton: high-gain lead tone at club volume, the birth of the modern "boutique" amp.
- **1972** -- *MXR Phase 90* -- a one-knob, four-stage phaser; its warm, swirling sweep becomes an instant studio shortcut and an Eddie Van Halen rhythm signature.
- **c. 1972** -- *Maestro Echoplex EP-3 (solid-state)* -- the tape-echo standard; players prize its **preamp** as much as its repeats, since the EP-3's input buffer adds a treble-forward "sparkle" coveted to this day.
- **1974** -- *Electro-Harmonix Electric Mistress* -- an early flanger in a box, its metallic jet-sweep heard across mid-seventies rock and reggae.
- **1976** -- *Boss CE-1 Chorus Ensemble* -- the first dedicated chorus pedal, derived from the Roland Jazz Chorus amp's circuit; lush, three-dimensional shimmer that defines clean tone for a decade.
- **1977** -- *Ibanez TS-808 Tube Screamer* (Overdrive Pro) -- a soft-clipping op-amp overdrive with a mid-hump; designed to push an already-cooking amp, it becomes the most imitated dirt pedal ever built.
- **1978** -- *Roland Jazz Chorus JC-120* -- solid-state, brutally clean, with stereo chorus built in; the reference clean platform for funk, post-punk, and pop.

## The 1980s

The decade of more: more gain, more compression, more rack gear, more chrome. The high-gain amp reaches its mature form -- a tight, saturated lead voice with a defined low end and a singing top -- and the first digital effects arrive, hinting that the whole signal chain might one day live inside a chip. Lead vocabulary leans on **modes** now: the **Aeolian** (natural minor) for dark rock, the **Dorian** (minor with a raised 6th) for a brighter blues-rock color, the **Phrygian** (minor with a b2) for the Spanish-tinged menace of metal.

- **c. 1980** -- *Mesa/Boogie Mark IIB/IIC and IIC+* -- the IIC+ (1983-85) becomes a holy grail for liquid, sustaining lead tone; Metallica and countless others chase it.
- **1981** -- *Marshall JCM800 (2203/2204)* -- a single-channel master-volume EL34 amp with cascaded gain; the no-nonsense crunch that powers eighties hard rock and, distorted further, thrash.
- **1983** -- *Boss DS-1 / and the early digital era* -- cheap, bright distortion in an orange box; meanwhile rack digital reverb and delay (Lexicon, Roland SDE-3000) reshape studio space.
- **1985** -- *Boss DD-2 Digital Delay* -- the first compact digital delay pedal; clean, long, repeatable echoes finally fit on a pedalboard.
- **1987** -- *Soldano SLO-100 (Super Lead Overdrive)* -- Mike Soldano's hand-built, high-gain 6L6 amp; smooth, harmonically dense saturation that influences nearly every "modded Marshall" boutique build to follow.
- **1989** -- *Dumble Overdrive Special (peak-era reputation)* -- Alexander Dumble's secretive, made-to-order amps (built since the late 1970s) reach mythic status; the touch-sensitive "D-style" clean-into-smooth-overdrive voice becomes a holy grail and a boutique-cloning industry.

## The 1990s

Two opposite impulses define the nineties. One reaches backward, reissuing tweed and plexi and germanium for players chasing vintage authenticity. The other reaches forward and asks the heretical question: what if the amplifier were *software*? The answer arrives at the very end of the decade and quietly begins to hollow out the tube amp's monopoly.

- **1991** -- *Matchless DC-30* -- a hand-wired, EL84 boutique amp reviving the Vox-style chime with modern build quality; the boutique-amp boom begins in earnest.
- **1992** -- *Marshall JCM900 and widespread reissue culture* -- diode-clipped higher gain on one hand; on the other, a market-wide hunger for **reissue** plexis, tweeds, and blackfaces.
- **1992** -- *Mesa/Boogie Dual Rectifier* -- a tube-rectified, scooped, saturated monster; the defining rhythm tone of nineties alt-metal and nu-metal.
- **1995** -- *Fulltone, Z.Vex, and the boutique-pedal wave* -- small builders reissue and refine germanium fuzz and transparent overdrive; the pedal becomes a connoisseur's object.
- **1996** -- *Line 6 AxSys 212 / Flextone* -- early digital-modeling combo amps; crude by later standards, but proof that DSP could imitate a tube amp's voice.
- **1998** -- *Line 6 POD* -- the kidney-bean modeler that put dozens of amp and cab "models" into a desktop box; it democratizes direct recording and makes "amp modeling" a household phrase.

## The 2000s

Digital modeling stops apologizing. The decade's leap is the **impulse response** -- a recording of exactly how a specific speaker cabinet and microphone color the sound -- combined with steadily better amp-circuit simulation. For the first time, a serious player could record an album direct and have listeners argue about whether they heard tubes or transistors.

- **2001** -- *Fractal Audio Axe-Fx (Standard)* -- a rackmount processor whose amp models and cab IRs aim at studio-grade realism; it becomes the high end of modeling.
- **2003** -- *Line 6 POD XT* -- expanded models and effects; modeling goes mainstream on stages and in bedrooms alike.
- **2007** -- *Kemper Profiler (development era)* -- Christoph Kemper pioneers "profiling": rather than model a circuit, the unit captures a sonic fingerprint of a real, mic'd amp; publicly unveiled in 2011.
- **2008** -- *Strymon (founding) and the DSP-pedal renaissance* -- studio-grade reverb, delay, and modulation in compact pedals; the stompbox catches up to the rack.

## The 2010s

Modeling and profiling become not just acceptable but, on many big tours and records, preferred -- lighter, quieter, infinitely recallable. The argument shifts from "can it sound real?" to "do I even need the real thing?" Tone debates move from tube types to firmware versions.

- **2011** -- *Kemper Profiler (commercial release)* -- profiling reaches the public; touring acts begin leaving real heads at home, carrying captured "profiles" instead.
- **2013** -- *Fractal Audio Axe-Fx II XL* -- a refinement widely regarded as a turning point where modeled tone satisfied a large share of professionals.
- **2015** -- *Line 6 Helix* -- a flexible, friendly floor modeler with a clear interface; modeling becomes genuinely easy to use, not just powerful.
- **2019** -- *Neural DSP Quad Cortex (announced)* -- a powerful quad-core floor unit with onboard "Neural Capture," promising profiling and modeling in one; it ships in 2021 and signals the machine-learning era of tone capture.

## The 2020s and Beyond

The newest tools blur the last lines. Neural networks now "learn" an amp from a short audio sample; cab IRs give way to dynamic, mic-position-modeled captures; and the humble overdrive pedal, after sixty years, is still being reissued, cloned, and revered. The instrument's tone is now equal parts physics and mathematics -- but the hand on the strings, the **vibrato** in a held note, the **bend** that lands a quarter-step sharp for tension and resolves home, still decides everything.

- **2021** -- *Neural DSP Quad Cortex (shipping)* -- neural-network capture brings machine learning into mainstream tone; a new front in the modeling-vs.-tubes conversation.
- **c. 2022-present** -- *AI/neural amp captures (NAM and others) go open-source* -- hobbyist and professional capture tools proliferate; the cost of a "real amp's fingerprint" approaches zero.
- **present** -- *Tube amps, boutique builders, and vintage reissues endure* -- the valve refuses to die; players keep choosing the feel and the air-moving push of a real speaker even as the digital tools grow indistinguishable on a recording.

---

Three short examples make the timeline audible. Each is original and illustrative -- play them on whatever rig you have and you will hear the decade it belongs to.

**Example 1 -- 1950s/early-1960s clean-archtop jazz-blues.** Moderate swing feel, ~120 BPM. Neck pickup, amp just breaking up.

```
e|------------------------------------------|
B|------------------------------------------|
G|--7b9r7--5----------5--7---------------5~--|
D|------------7--5--7------7--5--7~----7-----|
A|----------------------------------5--7----|
E|------------------------------------------|
```

*In the spirit of the ES-150: minor pentatonic in A with a half-step bend (b9r7) -- dial a warm neck pickup into an amp on the edge of breakup, tone rolled back, very little gain.*

**Example 2 -- 1980s JCM800 power-chord chug.** Steady eighths, ~132 BPM, heavy palm mute. EL34 amp, master volume up.

```
e|--------------------------------------------------|
B|--------------------------------------------------|
G|--------------------------------------------------|
D|--------------------------5--5--5--5--7--7--5-----|
A|--5--5--5--5--7--7--5------5--5--5--5--7--7--5-----|
E|--3--3--3--3--5--5--3------3--3--3--3--5--5--3-----|
```

*Root-fifth "5" chords on the bottom strings with tight PM (palm muting) on every note -- set the preamp high and the bass moderate so the low end stays defined rather than flubby, and let the EL34s tighten the attack.*

**Example 3 -- Late-1980s SLO-100/IIC+ liquid lead.** Free, vocal phrasing, ~80 BPM. High-gain lead channel, long delay behind it.

```
e|--15b17r15--12-------------------12~~~~~~~~~~~~~~--|
B|----------------15b17--13--15~~~------------------|
G|--------------------------------------------------|
D|--------------------------------------------------|
A|--------------------------------------------------|
E|--------------------------------------------------|
```

*Aeolian-flavored minor pentatonic phrasing in A with whole-step bends (b17r15) and wide, slow vibrato on the held notes -- the smooth, compressed high gain plus a subtle delay is what lets a single sustained note bloom and sing.*

> **Listen For**
>
> The whole century in five records. Hear the *clean valve power* on the Fender-tweed snap of Buddy Holly's "Peggy Sue" (1957). Hear *germanium fuzz that cleans up with the volume knob* on Jimi Hendrix's "Foxy Lady" from *Are You Experienced* (1967) -- a Fuzz Face into a cranked plexi. Hear *cascaded preamp gain and chorus shimmer* across Van Halen's *1984* (1984), Phase 90 swirl and "brown" saturation side by side. Hear *eighties high-gain rhythm precision* on Metallica's *Master of Puppets* (1986), the Mesa Mark-series chug at the heart of the riffs. And hear *modern modeling/profiling in the wild* on recent direct-recorded metal and pop -- much of it tracked through an Axe-Fx, Kemper, or Quad Cortex -- where the cab you "hear" is an impulse response and the amp is math, yet the bends and vibrato are still entirely human.
