# Appendix D - Recommended Listening: A Tone Discography

A book about tone can only point. The sound itself lives in the grooves, on the tape, in the bitstream — in the rooms where these records were cut and the speakers that moved the air. What follows is not a "greatest guitar solos" list. It is a map of *textures*, organized by the sound rather than the player, so that you can hear a single idea — a tweed amp folding in on itself, a Plexi opening its throat, a Big Muff swelling like an organ — traced across decades and genres until the character of the thing becomes unmistakable to your ear.

Listen on the best system you have, or good headphones. Many of these recordings predate stereo, or use it strangely; mono is often where the truth is. Where a year would be a guess, it has been left off rather than invented. Treat each entry as an invitation to put the needle down and *hear the circuit* described elsewhere in this book actually doing its work. Tone is learned the way language is — by immersion, by hearing it again and again until you stop noticing the words and start understanding the meaning.

## Tweed Breakup

The sound of a low-powered cathode-biased amp — a **Fender Tweed Deluxe** or Bassman — pushed until the power tubes sag and the note blooms into soft, vowel-like distortion. Warm, compressed, touch-sensitive: roll back the guitar volume and it cleans up.

- Neil Young — "Cinnamon Girl" — *Everybody Knows This Is Nowhere* (1969) — "Old Black" into a cranked Tweed Deluxe; hear the woolly low-mid saturation and the way the chords smear.
- The Rolling Stones — "Brown Sugar" — *Sticky Fingers* (1971) — Keith Richards' open-G Telecaster through small tweed-style amps; ragged, in-the-red rhythm tone.
- Larry Carlton — "Room 335" — *Larry Carlton* (1978) — a 335 into a Tweed-style combo; listen for the creamy edge-of-breakup that never fully distorts.
- Billy Gibbons — "La Grange" — *Tres Hombres* (1973) — spitting, harmonically dense tweed grind under the half-cocked-wah honk.

## Plexi Roar

The **Marshall** Super Lead and JTM45 lineage: EL34 power tubes, a 4x12 of Celestion Greenbacks, and the midrange shove that built hard rock. Aggressive, throaty, glorious when slammed.

- Jimi Hendrix — "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)" — *Electric Ladyland* (1968) — Strat into stacked 100-watt Marshalls; the sound is all teeth and air.
- Free — "All Right Now" — *Fire and Water* (1970) — Paul Kossoff's Les Paul into a Marshall; vibrato so vocal it sounds like a second singer.
- Led Zeppelin — "Whole Lotta Love" — *Led Zeppelin II* (1969) — Page's Les Paul and Marshall, thick and brown, the riff carved out of midrange.
- AC/DC — "Back in Black" — *Back in Black* (1980) — Angus and Malcolm Young, minimal gain, maximum air-moving Plexi authority.
- Van Halen — "Ain't Talkin' 'Bout Love" — *Van Halen* (1978) — the famous modified Plexi; saturated yet still snappy on the pick attack.

## Vox Chime & Jangle

The **Vox AC30**: EL84 power tubes, no negative feedback, a Top Boost circuit, and a treble that rings like struck glass. Bright, complex, harmonically rich — the sound of the British Invasion and everything jangly after it.

- The Beatles — "A Hard Day's Night" — *A Hard Day's Night* (1964) — the famous opening 12-string Rickenbacker chord, all AC30 chime and overtone.
- The Edge / U2 — "Where the Streets Have No Name" — *The Joshua Tree* (1987) — AC30 chime fed through a dotted-eighth delay; arpeggios shimmer and stack.
- R.E.M. — "Driver 8" — *Fables of the Reconstruction* (1985) — Peter Buck's Rickenbacker jangle, bright and ringing in the upper mids.
- Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers — "American Girl" — *Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers* (1976) — Mike Campbell's chiming Rickenbacker, all top-end sparkle.
- The Byrds — "Mr. Tambourine Man" — *Mr. Tambourine Man* (1965) — the 12-string Rickenbacker that launched jangle, compressed and bell-like.

## Blackface Clean & Spring Reverb

The **Fender Twin Reverb** and Deluxe Reverb of the mid-1960s: 6L6 power, scooped mids, glassy top, and the splash of a Hammond-built spring reverb tank. The benchmark American clean.

- Eric Johnson — "Cliffs of Dover" — *Ah Via Musicom* (1990) — violin-smooth lead, but listen to the bell-clear rhythm; pristine Fender clarity.
- The Beatles — "Don't Let Me Down" — *Past Masters* — watery, vibrato-laced clean Telecaster (John's, with a Fender amp), spacious and dimensional.
- Steely Dan — "Reelin' in the Years" — *Can't Buy a Thrill* (1972) — Elliott Randall's articulate, sparkling clean-into-light-crunch lead lines.
- John Mayer — "Slow Dancing in a Burning Room" — *Continuum* (2006) — modern blackface clean, every harmonic distinct, reverb deep and three-dimensional.

## Surf

Blackface clean's wilder cousin: a **Fender Showman** or Twin drenched in outboard spring reverb, single-coil Jaguars and Jazzmasters, fast tremolo picking, and a wet, cavernous splash on every note.

- Dick Dale — "Misirlou" — *Surfers' Choice* (1962) — the staccato single-string run, reverb crashing like surf, the founding document of the style.
- The Chantays — "Pipeline" — single (1963) — descending minor-key melody soaked in reverb, eerie and oceanic.
- The Surfaris — "Wipe Out" — single (1963) — driving, reverb-splashed rhythm under the famous drum break.
- Dick Dale — "Let's Go Trippin'" — single (1961) — arguably the first surf instrumental; hear the rhythmic chug and wet attack.

## Fuzz

Before overdrive there was *fuzz* — germanium transistors clipping a signal into ragged, sputtering square waves. The **Maestro FZ-1**, the **Sola Sound Tone Bender**, the **Fuzz Face**. Synthetic, buzzy, gated, and utterly distinct from tube distortion.

- The Rolling Stones — "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" — *Out of Our Heads* (1965) — Keith Richards' Maestro FZ-1 riff that put fuzz on the map.
- The Jimi Hendrix Experience — "Foxy Lady" — *Are You Experienced* (1967) — Fuzz Face into Marshall; the opening swell-into-bloom is pure germanium.
- The Beatles — "Think for Yourself" — *Rubber Soul* (1965) — fuzz bass (Paul, likely via a Tone Bender), gnarly and growling.
- Spirit — "I Got a Line on You" — *The Family That Plays Together* (1968) — bright, biting fuzz-rhythm hook.

## Wah

A foot-swept bandpass filter — the **Vox** and **Cry Baby** — sweeping a resonant peak across the midrange. Rock it for the vocal "wah-wah," or park it in one position for a fixed honk.

- Jimi Hendrix — "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)" — *Electric Ladyland* (1968) — the most famous wah intro ever recorded; hear the filter open and close.
- Cream — "White Room" — *Wheels of Fire* (1968) — Clapton's wah-drenched, almost-talking lead phrases.
- Isaac Hayes — "Theme from Shaft" — *Shaft* (1971) — Charles "Skip" Pitts' rhythmic 16th-note wah, the definitive funk pulse.
- Metallica — "Enter Sandman" — *Metallica* (1991) — Kirk Hammett's wah used as a tonal seasoning across the solo.

## Uni-Vibe

Four staggered photocells pulsing to mimic a Leslie rotating speaker — but lopsided, watery, and seasick in the best way. The **Univox Uni-Vibe** is part phaser, part chorus, all atmosphere.

- Jimi Hendrix — "Machine Gun" — *Band of Gypsys* (1970) — the Uni-Vibe wobble under sustained, screaming notes; the sound of a dive-bombing aircraft made musical.
- Robin Trower — "Bridge of Sighs" — *Bridge of Sighs* (1974) — the most luxuriant Uni-Vibe tone on record, thick and slow and oceanic.
- Pink Floyd — "Breathe (In the Air)" — *The Dark Side of the Moon* (1973) — Gilmour's lap-steel and guitar washed in slow modulation, dreamlike and floating.

## Big Muff Sustain

The **Electro-Harmonix Big Muff Pi**: a cascaded transistor fuzz with a scooped midrange and near-infinite, violin-like sustain. Huge, wooly, and singing.

- David Gilmour / Pink Floyd — "Comfortably Numb" — *The Wall* (1979) — the second solo: endless Muff sustain, every bend blooming and holding.
- The Smashing Pumpkins — "Cherub Rock" — *Siamese Dream* (1993) — layered Muffs creating a wall of saturated, scooped fuzz.
- Dinosaur Jr. — "Freak Scene" — *Bug* (1988) — J Mascis' ragged, sustaining Muff lead riding over the riff.
- Mudhoney — "Touch Me I'm Sick" — single (1988) — the Superfuzz-and-Muff filth that helped define the sound of a city.

## Tube-Screamer Blues

The **Ibanez Tube Screamer** (TS808/TS9): not a heavy distortion but a *push* — a mid-hump and soft clipping that tightens the low end and shoves a tube amp into singing overdrive.

- Stevie Ray Vaughan — "Pride and Joy" — *Texas Flood* (1983) — the TS into a cranked Fender; thick, mid-forward, snapping with pick attack.
- Stevie Ray Vaughan — "Texas Flood" — *Texas Flood* (1983) — slow-blues lead, every note fat and vocal, the Screamer feeding the amp's natural breakup.
- Gary Moore — "Still Got the Blues" — *Still Got the Blues* (1990) — Les Paul, overdrive, and a singing sustain rooted in the minor scale.
- John Mayer — "Gravity" (live, *Where the Light Is*, 2008) — a masterclass in dynamic overdrive that cleans and dirties with the picking hand.

## Tape Echo & Ambient Delay

Before digital, echo came from a loop of magnetic tape — the **Maestro Echoplex**, the **Roland Space Echo** — with wow, flutter, and a darkening of each repeat that no plugin fully replaces.

- The Edge / U2 — "Bad" (live, *Rattle and Hum*, 1988) — rhythmic delay used as a compositional engine, the repeats becoming part of the riff.
- Brian May / Queen — "Brighton Rock" — *Sheer Heart Attack* (1974) — multiple Echoplexes used to harmonize with himself in real time.
- Pink Floyd — "Time" — *The Dark Side of the Moon* (1973) — Gilmour's lead built around precisely timed repeats that fatten every phrase.
- The Shadows — "Apache" — single (1960) — Hank Marvin's early tape-echo melody, the template for echo-as-melody.

## Chorus & Flange (the 80s)

The decade of modulation: the **Boss CE-1** and **Roland Dimension D** chorus widening and shimmering a clean tone, the flanger adding a jet-engine sweep. Lush, glassy, and unmistakably of its era.

- The Police — "Message in a Bottle" — *Reggatta de Blanc* (1979) — Andy Summers' chorused add9 arpeggios, wide and watery.
- The Cure — "A Forest" — *Seventeen Seconds* (1980) — chorus and flange creating cold, cavernous space.
- Rush — "Spirit of Radio" — *Permanent Waves* (1980) — Alex Lifeson's chorus-soaked clean tones and chiming harmonics.
- Van Halen — "Unchained" — *Fair Warning* (1981) — a flanger thickening the famous crunch riff into something enormous.

## Mesa High Gain

The **Mesa/Boogie** revolution: a Fender lead channel cascaded into multiple gain stages, then refined into the Rectifier era. Tight, saturated, mid-rich, and built for sustain on demand.

- Carlos Santana — "Europa" — *Amigos* (1976) — the singing, sustaining "Boogie" lead tone that named the company's myth.
- Metallica — "Wherever I May Roam" — *Metallica* (1991) — the chunky, scooped rhythm tone that defined modern metal rhythm playing.
- Dream Theater — "Pull Me Under" — *Images and Words* (1992) — John Petrucci's articulate, high-gain precision; every fast note distinct.

## The Brown Sound

The contested, near-mythic tone of early **Van Halen** — a modified Marshall, allegedly run through a Variac to lower the wall voltage and sag the amp, though the exact recipe remains debated lore rather than settled fact. Brown, saturated, alive, three-dimensional.

- Van Halen — "Eruption" — *Van Halen* (1978) — the reference recording; hear the bloom, the sag, the harmonic richness on every tapped note.
- Van Halen — "I'm the One" — *Van Halen* (1978) — the rhythm tone showing the same brown saturation in motion.
- Van Halen — "Runnin' with the Devil" — *Van Halen* (1978) — big, open chords revealing the amp's three-dimensional depth.

A note on honesty: the "brown sound" is one of the most argued-over tones in rock. The Variac story, the exact amp modifications, and the role of the room and microphones are all part of long-running debate. Listen for the *result* — the saturated-yet-dynamic character — rather than chasing a single secret setting.

## Boutique High Gain

The post-Mesa refinement: small builders — **Bogner**, **Diezel**, **Soldano** — chasing tightness, low-end control, and articulation at extreme gain. Modern, surgical, enormous.

- Eric Clapton — "The Sky Is Crying" (live) — the **Soldano SLO-100** in Clapton's late-80s setup; smooth, saturated, vocal.
- Tool — "Schism" — *Lateralus* (2001) — Adam Jones' Diezel-driven tone, dark and tight with controlled low end.
- Steve Vai — "For the Love of God" — *Passion and Warfare* (1990) — soaring, ultra-sustaining high-gain lead with surgical clarity.

## Grunge Fuzz

The early-90s reaction against polish: cheap fuzz, the **Big Muff** and **Pro Co RAT**, loud-quiet-loud dynamics, and a deliberately ragged, unglamorous wall of distortion.

- Nirvana — "Smells Like Teen Spirit" — *Nevermind* (1991) — the Small Clone chorus on the clean verses and the RAT-and-amp roar on the chorus.
- Soundgarden — "Black Hole Sun" — *Superunknown* (1994) — shimmering, slightly seasick clean tones erupting into fuzz.
- Pearl Jam — "Alive" — *Ten* (1991) — Mike McCready's singing, sustaining lead over Stone Gossard's thick rhythm.
- Mudhoney — "Touch Me I'm Sick" — single (1988) — the blueprint: filthy, fuzzed-out, gleefully ugly.

## Delay as Composition

Where echo stops being an effect and becomes the architecture — the rhythmic, melodic, structural use of repeats, dotted eighths, and feedback as a writing tool.

- The Edge / U2 — "Where the Streets Have No Name" — *The Joshua Tree* (1987) — the dotted-eighth delay turns a simple arpeggio into a cathedral.
- Pink Floyd — "Run Like Hell" — *The Wall* (1979) — a stereo, dual-delay riff that exists only because of the timed repeats.
- Eric Johnson — "Manhattan" — *Ah Via Musicom* (1990) — cascading delays woven into the melodic line itself.
- Explosions in the Sky — "Your Hand in Mine" — *The Earth Is Not a Cold Dead Place* (2003) — post-rock delay as slow-building emotional structure.

## Modern Modeling

Digital profiling and modeling — the **Line 6 Helix**, **Fractal Axe-Fx**, **Kemper**, and **Neural DSP** — recreating amps, cabs, and microphone placements in software. Listen for how convincingly the modeled "amp in a room" now translates to record.

- Periphery — "Scarlet" — *Periphery II* (2012) — early, influential use of profiling/modeling for tight modern metal rhythm.
- Plini — "Electric Sunrise" — *Handmade Cities* (2016) — glassy cleans and smooth high gain, almost entirely in the digital domain.
- Polyphia — "Goose" — *New Levels New Devils* (2018) — pristine modeled cleans and articulate lead, hi-fi and modern.
- Tim Henson / Polyphia — "Playing God" — *Remember That You Will Die* (2022) — nylon-string and modeled electric blended with studio precision.

## Three Listening Studies

A discography is most useful when you stop sampling and start *comparing*. Pick a single variable and isolate it. Below are three short studies — with original, illustrative tab in the style of each era — designed to train your ear on one thing at a time. Play them yourself, then go back to the records and listen for the same move.

### Study 1 — Tweed vs. Plexi, same lick

The fastest way to *hear* a circuit is to play one phrase through two voices in your head. A tweed amp compresses and rounds; a Plexi shoves the midrange forward and stays articulate. The lick below sits in the **A minor pentatonic** scale (the five-note box every blues-rock player lives in: the root A, plus C, D, E, and G) with a classic **blue note** — the flatted fifth, E-flat — bent into. On a tweed, let the notes bloom and smear; on a Plexi, dig in and hear the attack survive.

```
Slow blues, ~70 bpm, heavy pick attack
e|--------------------------------------------|
B|--------------------------------------------|
G|------5b7r5--------------5------------------|
D|-7-------------7--5--7-------7--5-----------|
A|---------------------------------------7--5-|
E|--------------------------------------------|
   A minor pentatonic with bent b5 (E-flat)
```

*The same phrase will sound round and vocal through a cranked Tweed Deluxe and aggressive, mid-forward through a Plexi — dial the tweed with the amp near full and your guitar volume backed off; dial the Plexi wide open and ride the pick.*

### Study 2 — The jangle voicing

Vox chime is as much about *what you play* as the amp. The British-Invasion sound leans on open, ringing voicings — here an **add9** chord (a major triad with the 9th, the same note as the 2nd, added on top for sparkle without the tension of a full major-7). Let every string ring; the AC30's bright, feedback-free top end will turn the overtones into glass.

```
Bright, let ring -- AC30 Top Boost, treble up
e|--0----0----0----0--|
B|--3----3----0----0--|
G|--0----0----0----0--|
D|--2----0----2----2--|
A|--3----3----2----2--|
E|--0----0----0----0--|
   Gadd9 G/B  Em7  Em7
```

*An add9 is built root-3rd-5th-9th; voiced on the top strings into a chiming clean amp it shimmers rather than thickens — back off any gain, push the treble, and let the strings ring into each other.*

### Study 3 — Delay as composition

This is the dotted-eighth-note trick that built a thousand stadium anthems. With the delay set to a dotted-eighth of the tempo and fed back once or twice, you play *half* the notes and the repeats fill the gaps, so a simple line becomes a galloping, interlocking pattern. Play only the noteheads below; your delay supplies the rest.

```
Quarter = 120; delay = dotted-eighth, ~one repeat, ~40% mix
e|--12--------12--------12--------12--------|
B|--------13--------13--------13------------|
G|------------------------------------------|
D|------------------------------------------|
A|------------------------------------------|
E|------------------------------------------|
   Play noteheads only -- delay fills the gaps
```

*Set a digital or tape delay to a dotted-eighth (multiply your beat's millisecond value by 0.75) with one strong repeat; the echoes interleave with your picked notes so a slow arpeggio reads as a fast, rhythmic cascade — the engine behind countless ambient-delay riffs.*

The point of all of this is not to copy. It is to *recognize*. Once you can hear the difference between germanium fuzz and a tube screamer, between a blackface clean and a Vox chime, between a real tape echo and a digital one, you will never un-hear it — and every record you already love will open up another layer. The gear in this book made these sounds; these recordings are the proof.

> **Listen For**
>
> - **Tweed bloom:** Neil Young, "Cinnamon Girl" — the way the chords swell and compress rather than snap.
> - **Plexi midrange:** Free, "All Right Now" — Kossoff's vocal vibrato riding the amp's throaty mids.
> - **Vox chime:** The Beatles, "A Hard Day's Night" — the opening chord, all overtone and ring.
> - **Spring reverb splash:** Dick Dale, "Misirlou" — the wet, cavernous crash on every staccato note.
> - **Germanium fuzz:** The Rolling Stones, "(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction" — buzzy, gated, synthetic.
> - **Uni-Vibe wobble:** Jimi Hendrix, "Machine Gun" — the seasick pulse under the sustained screams.
> - **Big Muff sustain:** Pink Floyd, "Comfortably Numb" (second solo) — notes that bloom and refuse to die.
> - **Tube-screamer push:** Stevie Ray Vaughan, "Pride and Joy" — mid-forward, snapping, tightened low end.
> - **Dotted-eighth delay:** U2, "Where the Streets Have No Name" — the repeats becoming the riff itself.
